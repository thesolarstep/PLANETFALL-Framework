## Author's Note

PLANETFALL was not developed by a professionally trained astrophysicist.

I am a microbiologist by training, with research experience in microbiology, nanobiotechnology, data analysis, and scientific methodology. My entry into astronomy came through independent exploration of public astronomical datasets, particularly photometric and imaging archives.

This project emerged from a simple realization: scientific discovery often depends less on finding extraordinary signals and more on rigorously eliminating ordinary explanations.

The framework's philosophy was shaped by a failed anomaly investigation known as **Source 382**. That experience reinforced a lesson familiar across scientific disciplines: unusual results deserve skepticism before celebration.

As a result, PLANETFALL approaches candidate validation from an adversarial perspective. Rather than asking,

> "How do we find a planet?"

it asks,

> "How many ways can this candidate be proven wrong?"

The project reflects an outsider's attempt to apply principles of scientific auditing, reproducibility, and forensic analysis across disciplinary boundaries.

If the framework proves useful, it will not be because it assumes discoveries are real. It will be because it assumes they are not. All criticism, suggestions, and comments are welcome.
